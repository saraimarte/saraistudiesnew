# Valuation of Futures Contract

In the real world the futures price we end up locking in is the market price of the future's contract which is determined by supply and demand. 

You can find the market price of WTI Crude Oil Futures for example on the <a href = "https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/energy/crude-oil/light-sweet-crude.quotes.html">Quotes page in the CME Group Website</a> as the 'Last' Price.

As financial analysts, saying that supply and demand determines the price of the futures contract is kind of like a black box for us. 

So, we want to be able to have a concrete formula that we can actually use to calculate the value/price of a futures contract. 

Where can we start?

Well, we can ask ourselves, "what moves or drives these suppliers and demanders to want to buy or sell the future at a certain price?"

It could be...

**1. The current spot price of the underlying asset (S<sub>0</sub>):** This is the starting point of the value of a futures contract. Since a supplier could sell the asset for the spot price today, they will not agree to a future delivery for anything less than that current asset price, and demanders accept this as the starting point for their bid because they have no other choice than to accept what the sellers are offering, so the futures price will be at least the spot price.

**2. The risk-free rate (r):** High interest rates make futures more valuable because the buyer gets to keep their cash in the bank earning interest until the delivery date instead of spending it all on the spot today. Since they are making money on that cash in the meantime, the wait is actually profitable for them, so the price moves **up**.

**3. Storage costs (u):** High storage costs make futures more valuable because the buyer avoids the headache and expense of storing the physical goods themselves, so the price moves **up**.

**4. Dividends or coupons (q):** A high amount of cashflows or dividends for an asset makes futures less valuable because the person holding the future does not have access to those cash payments since they don't own the actual asset, so the price of the future moves **down**.

**5. Convenience yield (y):** A high convenience yield makes futures less valuable because a paper contract does't help you if you need the physical asset to keep your business running during a shortage. Since the 'future' version can't be used immediately like the spot version can, it trades at a discount, so the price moves **down**.

We can come up with a formula that captures all of these drivers of the futures price in order to do our best to estimate the value of a futures contract in a way that the supply and demand market would. 

It's an estimate because there could be other factors that we haven't thought of that could also affect the price of the future. 

Since, it's an estimate we call this a theoretical price of the future contract. 

This formula has a name- the cost of carry formula or model:

<RuleBox>
**Def.** The **Cost of Carry Model** is a futures pricing model (formula) that gives us the theoretical price of a futures contract based on the current spot price (S) of the underlying asset today (time 0) so S<sub>0</sub> and the costs (r, u) and benefits of holding the asset (q, y) until the delivery date T. 
</RuleBox>

<Latex formula="f_{0,T} = S_0 e^{(+r+u-q-y)T}"/>

# Contango and Backwardation

We can visualize the prices of different futures contracts over time. 

We call this a futures curve. 

<RuleBox>
**Def.** 
A futures curve is in **contango** if the price of the future is above the expected future spot price.
</RuleBox>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** 
A futures curve is in **backwardation** if the price of the future is below the expected future spot price. 
</RuleBox>

Contango means the future is more expensive (valuable) than the present. Backwardation means the present is more expensive (valuable) than the future.

Contango: The market is "calm." There's plenty of stuff. You pay extra for the "service" of someone else holding it for you until later.

Backwardation: The market is "panicked." There isn't enough stuff. You get a "discount" if you are willing to wait, because everyone else is fighting over the small amount available today.
