# Consumption vs Investment Assets 

**1. Real Assets:** Assets that have value of their own.   

**2. Financial Assets:** A claim on a real asset or the income generated by a real asset.  

We can also classify assets as either consumption or investment assets.

Note that investment assets can be either real or financial, but consumption assets are typically real assets.

**1. Consumption Assets:** Assets that are used for consumption or production purposes. 

**2. Investment Assets:** Assets that are used for investment purposes, meaning they are held with the expectation of generating a return or income in the future.

# Contango and Backwardation

We can visualize the prices of different futures contracts over time. 

We call this a futures curve. 

<RuleBox>
**Def.** 
A futures curve is in **contango** if the price of the future is above the expected future spot price.
</RuleBox>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** 
A futures curve is in **backwardation** if the price of the future is below the expected future spot price. 
</RuleBox>

Contango means the future is more expensive (valuable) than the present. Backwardation means the present is more expensive (valuable) than the future.

Contango: The market is "calm." There's plenty of stuff. You pay extra for the "service" of someone else holding it for you until later.

Backwardation: The market is "panicked." There isn't enough stuff. You get a "discount" if you are willing to wait, because everyone else is fighting over the small amount available today.

# How do we know what the futures price is? 

The futures price we end up agreeing to is the price on the market.

**1. The current spot price of the underlying asset:** This is the starting point of the negotiation. Since a supplier could sell the asset for cash today, they will not agree to a future delivery for anything less than that current market value, and demanders accept this as the starting point for their bid, so the futures price will be at least the spot price and the price moves **up**.
