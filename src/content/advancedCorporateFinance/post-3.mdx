**Description of the Company:** Clarkson Lumber is a rapidly growing lumber yard that experiences seasonal sales demand that peaks heavily during the summer months  

**Problem:** Growth => can't afford assets to support that growth

Mr. Clarkson is experiencing rapid, unsustainable sales growth- he is hitting the gas on the revenue line.  

<ul>
  <li><strong>Lack of internal funds:</strong> Because his profit margins are basically zero, his profits cannot fund the massive amount of new assets required to support his sales growth.</li>
  <li><strong>The costly discount:</strong> To make matters worse, he decides to take a 2% early payment discount from his suppliers. While this saves him about $68,000 a year, it forces him to pay his bills in 10 days instead of 40 days.</li>
  <li><strong>The financing gap:</strong> Paying suppliers 30 days earlier wipes out almost $400,000 in "free" financing from accounts payable. Because he is a private company and cannot simply issue equity, something else has to go up to fill that gap: Bank debt.</li>
</ul>

Clarkson is currently asking the bank for $750,000 upfront. 

If he guesses wrong and takes too little, he will inevitably have to go crawling back to the bank to say, "Yeah, I need more money." 

This puts Clarkson in a terrible position because the bank will have all the power to squeeze him.  

# The Solution: Pro Forma Forecasting

<ul>
  <li>To ensure he doesn't have to go back and ask for more money, we need to figure out exactly how much money Clarkson actually needs before going to the bank.</li>
  <li>To do this, you have to build a "pro forma" forecast.</li>
  <li>This involves setting up the balance sheet and calculating the required external financing—which acts as a plug variable, or X, in the notes payable/revolver account.</li>
</ul>

<RuleBox>

**Def.** **Pro-Forma** means adjusting for change so doing a **Pro-Forma Forecast** means you are making a specific change to the business and updating your financial expectations to reflect that change.   

</RuleBox>

In the Clarkson Lumber case, the pro forma adjustment is the decision to take the early payment discount. Instead of letting accounts payable grow naturally driven by purchases, you force a change by locking it in at 10 days of payables.

# The 5 Steps of Pro Forma Forecasting

<ul>
  <li><strong>Forecast the Income Statement:</strong> You always start a financial model by forecasting the income statement. You do this to calculate your projected retained earnings (Net Income).</li>
  <li><strong>Calculate Next Period's Equity (E):</strong> Next period's equity is equal to last period's equity plus those new retained earnings. Once you have this calculation, you know E.</li>
  <li><strong>Forecast Assets (A):</strong> Next, you forecast your assets as a percentage of sales. Once you do this, you know A.</li>
  <li><strong>Forecast Liabilities (L):</strong> Then, you forecast all of the liabilities except for the revolving line of credit (the notes payable). You leave the notes payable as an X because you do not know what it is yet. At this point, you know all of your assets, all of your equity, and all of your liabilities except for X.</li>
  <li><strong>Solve for Bank Debt (X):</strong> Finally, you use the fundamental accounting equation by setting assets equal to liabilities plus equity. You then solve the algebra equation for X to find the exact amount of bank debt needed.</li>
</ul>
