**Moral of the case: Product market policy and financial policy must jibe**

# Massey Ferguson's Product Market Strategy 

 <RuleBox>
 **Def.** A **product market strategy** is a plan for positioning, pricing, and promoting a product to its target audience.  
 </RuleBox>

Massey Ferguson's product  market strategy was to focus on 3<sup>rd</sup> world countries for growth- banking on these countries popping economically in the future. 

**What happens when they pop economically?**

Businesses move in, new investments in buildings, and what do you need to make way for all of that? Tractors.

So Massey would be the one there to offer those tractors. 

This strategy is even more sensible because they're going where competition is weak. 

**Why is competition weak in these 3<sup>rd</sup> world countries?**

One reason is expropriation risk. 

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **Expropriation** is the act of a sovereign government seizing a foreign company's local assets, such as factories or land, often without providing fair compensation. 
</RuleBox>

Since Massey's product market strategy consisted of expanding into least developed countries, these countries could expropriate Massey's assets.  

So there was a huge expropriation risk for Massey.

What did Massey do in order to eliminate this risk?

Massey Ferguson implemented a financial strategy of borrowing money directly from the host governments to fund their local facilities.  

The host government became a lender to Massey, giving them a financial incentive to see the company succeed rather than shut it down and hence reducing the risk of the government seizing Massey's assets.  

Also, because the government was financially tied to Massey, they were incentivized to block competitors like John Deere from entering the market, effectively protecting Massey's position.
