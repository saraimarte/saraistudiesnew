# Intro 

The skill we need to learn in this lesson is how to value a firm's equity.

First, let's go over the definition of equity. 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** **Equity** is the remaining dollar value of a firm that belongs to the owners (shareholders) after all liabilities and debts have been paid in full.  
</RuleBox>

## Why would we want to value a firm's equity?

**1. We can use it to avoid bad stock investments** by determining if the stock is under or over valued in comparison to what we got as equity. 

**2. We can use it to make acquisition decisions** because there is a formula (we will learn later) that calculates how much you need to buy a firm and that formula includes equity.

**3. We can use it to price new shares in an IPO** because if there is no price the market deems the shares worth then the starting point is what the company thinks the shares are worth which is equity.  

There are two main approaches we will cover to value equity: (a) Income/Discounted Cash Flows (Income) Approach and the (b) Market/Comps Approach. 

First, we will cover the income approach to value equity which consists of two approaches in itself: (a) the direct approach and (b) the indirect approach.

# A. The Income Approach 

The idea of the income approach is to value equity based on income (cash flows) that will be received, in the future. 

Now, if the income being measured is dividends then we call that using the direct approach. 

If the income being measured is unlevered free cash flow then we call that using the indirect approach.  

# 1 | Direct Approach

The idea of this approach is to value the equity of a firm based on how much the cash flows the equity holders will be paid are worth *today*.

What cash flows do equity holders receive?
 
<Pop t = "cash the firm has leftover, that they decide to pay to shareholders">Dividends</Pop> and <Pop t = "profit you gain when you sell an asset for a higher price than what you paid for it.">capital gains.</Pop> 

However, we can ignore capital gains as cash flows because of assumption 1 (see below.)

<DropDown title = "DCF Assumptions">

In the DCF approach we assume, 

**1. that shareholders will buy a stock and hold it forever** (because of this assumption we don't have to worry about capital gains as cash flows to equity holders because there will never be a profit made if the shareholder doesn't sell the stock!)

**2. that eventually the firm's dividends will start growing at a constant rate** (if we don't make this assumption then we would be stuck forecasting dividends every year until infinity (b/c of assumption 1) so we would have an infinite stream of future dividends which is mathematically impossible to computer so we have to assume that at some point the dividends will grow at a constant rate and that will allow us to convert those infinite stream of dividends into one formula we can solve.)

**3. that managers will find an optimal dividend policy and act optimally by adhering to that policy** (we make this assumption because it's nearly impossible to find the <Pop t = "the optimal amount of dividends to pay and when"> optimal dividend policy</Pop> and there is no way to force managers to pay those dividends as per the policy.)   

**4. that the firm's dividends will be paid at the end of the year**
</DropDown>

Overall there are two elements of DCF: (a) the cash flow and (b) discount rate used.

# 2 | Indirect Approach

In order to do the indirect approach we need to know two things:

**1.** The definition of enterprise value    
**2.** Two ways to find enterprise value 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** **Enterprise Value** is how much you need to buy a firm. 

Specifically, it's the sum of all financial claims against the firm (because you're not just buying the firm, you are buying their obligations as well like the debt they owe, and the common and preferred stock that represent the ownership interests you must buyout to have 100% control over the firm’s future cash flows.)

Note that if the firm has excess cash when you buy it, you can use that cash to cover the debt and PS costs so it takes away from the <Pop t = "Enterprise Value">cost of buying a firm.</Pop> So we have, 

**Enterprise Value = Equity (Common Stock) + Preferred Stock + Debt - Excess Cash**

</RuleBox>

Note that the Enterprise Value formula has equity as a term so if we solve the equation for equity we get: 

**Equity (Common Stock) = Enterprise Value - Preferred Stock - Debt + Excess Cash**

Now, if we can find what Enterprise Value, Preferred Stock, Debt and Excess Cash are- we can get a value for equity! 

This is called the indirect approach to calculating equity. 

But in order to get equity in this formula we need to know what enterprise value is so we need to know another way to find enterprise value:

Recall that enterprise value is the sum of all the financial claims against the firm which we can represent by keeping track of the amount that is available for these <Pop t = "aka capital providers"> people who have financial claims against the firm.</Pop>

This value is called Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF) or Unlevered Free Cash Flow (ULFCF.)

## Why are we using Unlevered Free Cash Flow to the Firm?

If you want to value anything, you have to look at the cash it will put into the owner's (or whoever benefits from the asset) pocket. For example, 

<ul>
<li>If you want to value a bond, you look at the interest payments it will generate for the owner (bond holder).</li>

<li>If you want to value a stock, you look at the dividends it will generate for the owner (stock holder)  </li>

<li>If you want to value the enterprise, you look at the **cash it will generate for the <Pop t = "all the capital providers">owner + beneficiaries</Pop>** (b/c debt holders are not owners but they do benefit from all the cash generated to pay them.)</li>

</ul>

We call this "**cash the firm will generate for the <Pop t = "all the capital providers">owner + beneficiaries</Pop>**", unlevered free cash flows or free cash flows to the firm. 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** **Unlevered Free Cash Flows (UFCF)** is the cash left over after the company has already kept what it needs to operate so it's the cash leftover for all <Pop t = "people who give money to the firm (equity holders & debt holders)"> capital providers</Pop> (the people the firm has financial obligations to.)
</RuleBox>

# WACC as the Discount Rate 

Again, in the income/DCF approach, whether you are using dividends or UFCF as the cash flows, there is also another element of DCF and that is the discount rate we need to use. 

In these methods, we use WACC as the discount rate. 

<RuleBox>
**Def.** The **Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)** is is the minimum annual percentage return a company must generate on its investments to <Pop t = "aka keep the lenders and shareholders happy"> satisfy the required returns of its lenders and shareholders.</Pop>  

<Latex formula={`WACC = (1 - t)k_d \\left( \\frac{D}{V} \\right) + k_e \\left( \\frac{E}{V} \\right)`} />

**k<sub>d</sub>:** cost of debt     
**k<sub>e</sub>:** cost of equity   
**(D/V):** weight of debt (tells you the % of the firm that is funded by debt)  
**(E/V):** weight of equity (tells you the % of the firm that is funded by equity)  
**D:** debt   
**E:** equity  
**V:** debt + equity   
**t:** tax rate   
</RuleBox>

<iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/E5nzWERmQ5c?si=aOqqh__xhk8gv8nO&amp;start=5" title="YouTube video player" frameborder="0" allow="accelerometer; autoplay; clipboard-write; encrypted-media; gyroscope; picture-in-picture; web-share" referrerpolicy="strict-origin-when-cross-origin" allowfullscreen></iframe>

**1. Why do we use WACC as the discount rate?**

Recall, the formula for present value at time 0 (today) is

<Latex formula={`PV_0 = \\frac{CF_T}{(1 + r)^T} = \\frac{\\text{Cashflow}}{\\text{Discount}}`} />

<br/>

<ul>
<li>**Cashflow:** In this case, when we are valuing an enterprise, the cashflow is Unlevered Free Cash Flow (UFCF) which is the cash that goes to capital providers (both lenders and shareholders).</li>

<Latex formula={`PV_0 = \\frac{CF_T}{(1 + r)^T} = \\frac{\\text{Cashflow}}{\\text{Discount}} = \\frac{\\text{UFCF}}{\\text{?}}`} />

<br/>

<li>**Rule:** You the numerator and denominator to match the entity you're dealing with. In this case, the entity is the capital providers. So, **the cash in the numerator must belong to the same people whose costs are in the denominator.**</li>

<Latex formula={`PV_0 = \\frac{CF_T}{(1 + r)^T} = \\frac{\\text{Cashflow}}{\\text{Discount}} = \\frac{\\text{Cash belonging to Capital Providers}}{\\text{Cost of the Capital Providers}}`} />

<br/>

<li>**Discount:** Therefore, we want the denominator to be the *cost* *of* capital providers (both lenders and shareholders) and what is the cost of capital providers that the firm incurs? WACC!</li>

<Latex formula={`PV_0 = \\frac{CF_T}{(1 + r)^T} = \\frac{\\text{Cashflow}}{\\text{Discount}} = \\frac{\\text{UFCF}}{\\text{WACC}}`} />

</ul>

By definition, WACC is the weighted average cost of capital- capital which the capital providers... provide. 

<Latex formula={`PV_0 = \\frac{CF_T}{(1 + r)^T} = \\frac{\\text{Cashflow}}{\\text{Discount}} = \\frac{\\text{Cash belonging to Capital Providers}}{\\text{Cost of the Capital Providers}} = \\frac{\\text{UFCF}}{\\text{WACC}} `} />

## Practice

Q: T/F PV of UFCF discounted back at the cost of equity (k<sub>e</sub>) gives you enterprise value.

<Solution>
**False.** The cost of equity is the cost of getting capital from equity holders *only* and that doesn't match the numerator which includes cash belonging to equity holders *and* lenders. The denominator needs to be the cost of both equity holders *and* lenders. 
</Solution>

Q: T/F PV of UFCF discounted back at the cost of debt (k<sub>d</sub>) gives you enterprise value.

<Solution>
**False.** The cost of debt is the cost of getting capital from lenders *only* and that doesn't match the numerator which includes cash belonging to lenders *and* equity holders. The denominator needs to be the cost of both lenders *and* equity holders. 
</Solution>


Q: T/F PV of UFCF discounted back at the WACC gives you enterprise value.

<Solution>
**True.** The WACC is the cost of getting capital from *both* equity holders and lenders and that *does* match the numerator which includes cash belonging to *both* equity holders *and* lenders. 
</Solution>

Q: T/F When valuing enterprise value, the cashflow being discounted is UFCF. 

<Solution>
**True.** 
</Solution>

Q: T/F When valuing equity using the direct income approach, the cashflow being discounted is dividends. 

<Solution>
**True.** 
</Solution>


Q: T/F PV of dividends discounted back at the cost of equity gives you the <Pop t = "or the value of equity">equity.</Pop>

<Solution>
**True.** The cost of equity is the cost of getting capital from equity holders and the numerator is the amount going to equity holders so they match!
</Solution>

Q: T/F PV of dividends discounted back at the WACC gives you equity. 

<Solution>
**False.** In the numerator, we have dividends which is the amount going to equity holders so in the denominator we would want the cost of getting capital from equity holders and that is not WACC. WACC is the cost of getting capital from equity holders *and* lenders. Hence, the denominator doesn't match the numerator so WACC is not the right discount rate.  
</Solution>

Q: T/F PV of dividends discounted back at the WACC gives you the cost of equity. 

<Solution>
**False.** Two things wrong here. 

First, taking the Present Value (PV) of a cashflow gives you a Value (a dollar amount)-not a cost- of that cash flow today so it won't give you the *cost* of equity. 

Second, the discount rate is incorrect for the same reasons as the previous question.
</Solution>

# DCF Limitation

<IB>
Traditional DCF **undervalues** companies.  
</IB>


Up until now, we have assumed cash flows are happening at the end of the year. 

This is okay for retail or toy companies which make most of their cash at the end of the year because of the holiday shopping season but for other companies such as hotel chains or McDonald's- ones that generate cashflow smoothly throughout the year not just at the end- using traditional DCF as a method to value them is doing them a disservice because there is another approach that will make their company look more valuable (rightly so) and it's called the Mid-Year Convention Approach. 

# Mid-Year Convention 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** **Mid-Year Convention** A valuation technique used in Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) modeling that treats cash flows as if they are received in the exact middle of the year (e.g., June) rather than at the end.  
</RuleBox>

<Note>
Mid-Year Convention is not really another approach or method to value equity, it's more a technique that you can use in any of the income approaches. 
</Note>

## How does Mid-Year Convention Approach Increase the Value of a Firm?

Mid-Year Convention method shortens the <Pop t = "length of discounting period" >time it takes to receive a cash flow </Pop> from 1 year to 0.5 years (6 months.)

And conveniently the less time you have to wait for cash flow, the higher the value of the cash flow today. 

## Who Prefers Which Approach? 

So, someone who wants to sell a company, and would want to make their company look more valuable, would use the mid-year convention approach because it *will* make the company look more valuable. 

However, whether that value is fair or misleading depends the type of firm: 

<ul>

    <li>If they use the mid-year convention approach on a retail company, it would be assuming the company gets all it's cash flows at the middle of the year, which we all know is not true (they get most of their cash during the holiday season) so it *would* make the retail company more valuable but ... it would be misleading because the assumption is wrong. </li>

    <li>If they use it on a hotel then it would be assuming the hotel gets it's cash flows in the middle of the year, which is fair to assume because a hotel generally earns its cash flows smoothly throughout the year (half before June, half after), or has its peak season right in the summer (June), so the approach would make the hotel more valuable but rightly so. </li>

</ul>

On the other side of the coin, you have the buyer, who would prefer if the company was valued using DCF because it would then be undervalued so they would be paying less than they would if it was valued with the use of mid-year convention technique.

# B. Market/Comparables Approach 

Again, there are two main approaches to value equity: (a) Income/Discounted Cash Flows (Income) Approach and the (b) Market/Comps Approach.

We have so far covered the Income Approach, now we will cover the Market Approach. 

The reality is that no one in industry closes a deal just on DCF because it's based mostly on *your* assumptions- it's your opinion! 

You can make DCF spit out almost any valuation you want just by tweaking the WACC, for example, which is based on your assumptions. 

Instead, DCF is used more as a ballpark estimate on what the value would be but it's more realistic to ask what the market would be willing to pay right now based on what it pays for similar companies. 

Just like the income approach, the market or comparables approach has two approaches within itself: (a) Public Company Comparables Method/Trading Comparables Method and (b) Precedent Transactions Comparables Method. 

We will only cover the first but it is important to know what they are and the difference between them. 

**1. Trading Comparables Analysis:** You look for comparable companies that are currently trading on the public stock market to see what everyday investors are paying for a **minority stake** of their shares.

**2. Precedent Transactions Analysis:** You look for comparable companies that were recently bought in M&A deals to see what acquirers actually paid for a **majority stake** to take over the whole business.

The **only difference** between the two is that in the precedent transaction method **you have to pay a premium for the equity if you want control.**

# Trading Comparables Approach 

The idea of this approach is to value a minority stake in a firm by comparing it to a <Pop t = "similar">*comparable*</Pop> firm by using financial metrics such as <Pop t = "ratios">multiples</Pop> to compare them (which we also call comparables or comps.) 

The idea of the use of multiples is if a comparable company trades at a certain price relative to its earnings (or revenue, or EBITDA), then a truly similar company should trade at roughly the same ratio. 

# What is a Valid Multiple

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** A **Valid Multiple** is one where the cash flow in the denominator and what we are trying to value in the numerator are in agreement with one another.    
</RuleBox>

<ul>
<li>For example, **price/earnings** is a valid multiple because the numerator is the price one share of stock and the denominator is earnings which only equity owners have a claim on so both the top and the bottom match perfectly because they only deal with Equity.</li>

<li>But **price/sales** is not because numerator is price per one share of stock but the denominator is sales which equity holders are not the only ones that have a claim to those sales so the numerator and denominator don't match.</li>

</ul>

## The Two Most Popular Multiples 

**1. Price/Sales Multiple:** this is a revenue multiple (meaning it has revenue/sales in the denominator.)

Revenue multiples are **useful when earnings are negative** or when the firm is overall inefficient (e.g. government owned enterprise.)

In order for two companies to trade at the same revenue multiple, the projected profit margins should look similar over time (e.g. you can't use Chanel to value Michael Kors because even though they're in the same industry and have similar business model- their profit margins are way different.)

Bodegas, Restaurants, Consulting Company or companies that have little to no inventory are traded at price/sales. 

**2. EV/EBITDA Multiple:** will give us an estimate of the firm's enterprise value (EV.) 

The higher the multiple, the higher the firm is valued at. 

In order to use this multiple you want to pick what we call **comparables** which are companies that are similar to the one you want to value. 

What similar factors do we look for? Well we look for if they are in the/have... 

<ul>
    <li>Same Industry?</li>
    <li>Similar locations?</li>
    <li>Same Target Customer?</li>
    etc.
</ul>

Once we have a group of chosen comparables, to find ones that truly match we look for the four factors-that influence the multiple- to be very close.

What are those factors?

**The EV/EBITDA multiple is a function of four factors:**

**1. Growth (+):** how fast the company is increasing its revenues and profits.  

**2. Reinvestment (-):** how much cash the firm <Pop t = "invests"> puts back</Pop> into its operations (e.g. like buying new equipment) 

**3. Tax (-):** how much taxes the firm pays

**4. Risk (-):** how uncertain we are about the firm's future cash flows. 

The above are four drivers we can pull that has an effect on EV/EBITDA multiple. 

All the drivers decrease (-) the multiple except for growth which increases (+) it. 

**The practical use of these four drivers:** 

Again, when selecting comparable companies, you want companies that match your target on all four dimensions. 

When negotiating <Pop t = "(which will be determined by the EV/EBITDA multiple)">price</Pop> as a seller, these are the levers that you can pull. 

You can say "our growth is faster" or "we pay less taxes than these companies" to argue for a multiple above the <Pop t = "the avg EV/EBITDA of the companies you're comparing yours too">comp average</Pop> which will then increase your EV/EBITDA multiple which will then increase the price someone is willing to pay for the firm. 

Using an enterprise multiple is an indirect way to value equity, working exactly the same way we did in the indirect method of the income approach (where you find Enterprise Value first, then subtract debt to reach equity). 

The only difference is how we calculate that EV: instead of discounting future UFCF, we simply multiply our firm's EBITDA by the average EV/EBITDA multiple of our comparable companies. 

Note that in this class, we only compare our company to one comp not a group of them so it will just require us multiplying the comp's EV/EBITDA by our company's EBTIDA to get our company's EV and then from there it's the same as the indirect income approach where we use the EV formula to solve for our company's equity.

# 1 | Calculating Last Twelve Month's (LTM) Numbers 

In the market trading comps approach, we deal with the EV/EBITDA ratio and it requires us to find a *year's worth* of EBITDA- not just *any* year, not the last fiscal year but the *most recent* 12 months.

The only form that gives us a years worth of data is the 10-K. 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** The **10-K** is what we call the annual financial report that public companies are legally required to file with the SEC that contains the 12 months worth of financial data ending in their <Pop t="the month the company ends their year at">fiscal year end.</Pop>

</RuleBox>

The problem with the 10-K is that it gives us financial data ending in their fiscal year end (which can be any month of the year.) 

E.g. So if today is April 2026 and a firm has a fiscal year ending January then if you go look up their most recent 10-K it will only give you 12 months worth of data *ending* in January but you need 12 months worth of data ending today! April!

So between January and April you have missing data. 

Where can you find this data? 

The 10Q!

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** The **10-Q** is what we call the quarterly financial reports that public companies are required to file with the SEC that contain the most recent financial data since the 10-K filings.
</RuleBox>

**Fact**. A financial quarter is 3 months long, but a company only files three 10-Qs per year. The fourth quarter is never filed as a 10-Q; instead, those last 3 months are rolled directly into the annual 10-K.

**Fact**. It Contains not only the first quarter of this year but last year's too.  

E.g. So, again, if today is April 2026 and let's say I want LTM worth of Revenue and my fiscal year ends in January. 

<ul>
    <li>**Step 1:** Start with the 10-K (+12 months worth of revenue). Take the total Revenue from the most recent 10-K ending January 2026.</li>
    <li>**Step 2:** Add the current 10-Q (+3 months worth of revenue). Add the Revenue from the newest Q1 10-Q (Feb, Mar, Apr 2026). You now have 15 months of data.</li>
    <li>**Step 3:** Subtract the prior year's 10-Q (-3 months worth of revenue). Subtract the Revenue from last year's Q1 10-Q (Feb, Mar, Apr 2025).</li>
    <li>**Result:** Exactly the *last* 12 months of revenue ending April 2026 (today). You have successfully swapped out the old 3 months for the new 3 months. </li>
</ul>

<Latex formula={`\\text{LTM} = \\text{Most Recent 10-K} + \\text{Current 10-Q} - \\text{Prior Year's Matching 10-Q}`} />

We did this for revenues, but if we do the same steps for every line item we need to calculate EBTIDA, we will be able to find LTM EBITDA by using the LTM components of EBITDA. 

## When is the only time you don't need to find LTM for your data?

If the 10-K is the most recent financial statement (meaning there hasn't been any other filings like 10Qs) then you don't need to calculate LTM values- you can just use the 12 months worth of data from the 10-K. 

# 2.1 | Why we Need to Use Run-Rate Cash Flows 

When valuing equity using trading comps (market approach), like EV/EBITDA for example, we rely on getting EBITDA from the books ... and not *just* EBITDA but LTM EBITDA and not *just* LTM EBITDA but LTM Run-rate EBITDA. 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** **Run-Rate Cash flows** are a firm's "normal," repeating cash flows, calculated by taking their reported numbers and deleting any unusual, one-time events (like a lawsuit payout or selling a factory) that won't happen again next year.
</RuleBox>

**Another Def.** A **Run-Rate Number** is a reliable and reproducible level of cash flow that you can use to serve as the true baseline for all your future projections and valuation models.

If we calculated an EBITDA that takes into account restructuring charges, an impairment, a lawsuit, *and* the selling of a factory, for example, then that EBITDA is no good! 

Why?

Because it took into account charges that won't be happening again. 

We need to use *run-rate* numbers- ones that will be accurate in the in the future, serving as a reliable baseline for what the business will actually generate going forward not numbers that were affected by one-time events. 

# Calculating Run-Rate Cash Flows (Scrubbing)

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** **Scrubbing** is the act of cleaning or *scrubbing* a financial number by removing any unusual, one-time charges or <Pop t = "makes the number bigger like selling a factory makes EBITDA higher">windfalls</Pop>, to reveal what the true number is under regular conditions. 
</RuleBox>

<ul>
<li>**Where to find these unusual items?:** footnotes or MD&A in the financial statements or in the statement of cash flows under 'Adjustments to reconcile net earnings to total cash provided by operating activities'.</li>
<li>You want to add back any unusual expenses.</li>
<li>You want to subtract any unusual windfalls.</li>
</ul>

## 2.2 | What happens if there is an Acquisition or Divestiture?

<ul>
<li>If there is an <Pop t = "the selling/buying of a company">acquisition</Pop> or a <Pop t ="disposing of a business unit, subsidiary, product line or company assets">divestiture</Pop> that occurs midway through the year, then you should read the MD&A for how it was accounted for (in the U.S. under GAAP, acquisitions are accounted for using the **purchase accounting method.**) </li>
</ul>

<RuleBox align="left">
**Def.** **Purchase Accounting** says that a company can only report the financial results of an acquired firm for the exact months it officially owned them.
</RuleBox>

E.g. You are Firm A. Your fiscal year end is December. 

<ul>
<li>**The Problem**: If you buy Firm B in September, your December 10-K will only include 4 months of their EBITDA because you've only legally owned them for 4 months. The first 8 months legally vanish. Because EBITDA represents the true earning power of the business, missing 8 months of it **makes your combined LTM EBITDA look artificially low** and severely undervalues your company's actual earning potential.</li>

<li>**The Solution:** As finance majors, we don't care about the accounting timeline; we care about projecting reality. The reality is that because Firm A acquired Firm B, Firm A now has more earning power and our EBITDA should reflect that. To capture that true earning power, we simply step in and add back those missing 8 months of Firm B's EBITDA so our valuation models reflect what the combined machine will actually generate in a full, normal year.</li>
</ul>
