# About Clarkson Lumber 

<h1 style = "color:red">ⓘ Moral of the case: Profitability ≠ Liquidity</h1>

<ul>
  <li>**Company Description:** Clarkson Lumber was a <Pop t = "Business to Consumer (B to C) aka they sold to regular people and not businesses" >retail</Pop> lumber yard that experienced seasonal sales demand that peaked heavily during the summer months.</li>

  <li>**Sales Growth:** Clarkson Lumber was rapidly growing in sales about 25% per year and expected growth to keep going at that rate.</li>

  <li>**Location Advantage (1):** The lumber yard was located in Oregon during a time when there was a massive movement of people fleeing California due to high taxes and high real estate prices. Since Oregon's real estate was cheaper, there was a steady influx of people moving in and building homes and where would they buy the materials they needed? From Clarkson. These macroeconomic factors were incredibly favorable, putting Mr. Clarkson in the absolute right place at the right time to capture a naturally booming demand for lumber.</li>

  <li>**Location Advantage (2):** The lumberyard was right at the railroad tracks which means that they were saving on supply chain costs because the trains would just stop at Clarkson Lumber, unload the lumber and Clarkson would take it in. They didn't need to pay trucks to bring them the lumber.</li>

  <li>**Lower Price Advantage:** Because they saved on supply chain costs, they were able to keep prices lower than normal. This meant that they were solely competing on lowest price.</li>
</ul>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **Revenue Vacuum** is a predatory market event where a large, well-capitalized competitor (like Home Depot) enters a new area and temporarily slashes their prices. This strategy simultaneously sucks up all current demand and incentivizes customers to accelerate their future purchases to take advantage of the discount, thereby starving local competitors of cash flow for months and driving them out of business.
</RuleBox>

If Home Depot does perform a revenue vacuum on Clarkson then it's even worse for them because Clarkson competes only on lowest price and if they are no longer the lowest cost producer then they are ... nothing. Gone. 

**How do you survive a revenue vacuum?** 

A: Keep expenses low. Ofcourse Clarkson could have to pay monthly bills, overhead, or leases but if they have to pay interest on top of that (which they do with their 73% leverage ratio) - the speed at which they are going to decline and die will accelerate tremendously.

If they want to survive that, **they need equity.** They need to raise money to get rid of the debt. 

**But... who's going to fund them?** 

There are no barriers to entry and they are only competing on low prices- who in their right mind would invest in this company? 

**No one!** So it's too late for Clarkson to raise equity.

**Now, again, why is he so desperate for equity in the first place?** 

**To get rid of debt.**
