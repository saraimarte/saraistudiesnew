# Calculating Last Twelve Month's (LTM) Numbers 

In the market trading comps approach, we deal with the EV/EBITDA ratio and it requires us to find a *year's worth* of EBITDA- not just *any* year, not the last fiscal year but the *most recent* 12 months.

The only form that gives us a years worth of data is the 10-K. 

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** The **10-K** is what we call the annual financial report that public companies are legally required to file with the SEC that contains the 12 months worth of financial data ending in their <Pop t="the month the company ends their year at">fiscal year end.</Pop>

</RuleBox>

The problem with the 10-K is that it gives us financial data ending in their fiscal year end (which can be any month of the year.) 

E.g. So if today is April 2026 and a firm has a fiscal year ending January then if you go look up their most recent 10-K it will only give you 12 months worth of data *ending* in January but you need 12 months worth of data ending today! April!

So between January and April you have missing data. 

Where can you find this data? 

The 10Q!

<RuleBox align = "left"> 
**Def.** The **10-Q** is what we call the quarterly financial reports that public companies are required to file with the SEC that contain the most recent financial data since the 10-K filings.
</RuleBox>

**Fact**. A financial quarter is 3 months long, but a company only files three 10-Qs per year. The fourth quarter is never filed as a 10-Q; instead, those last 3 months are rolled directly into the annual 10-K.

E.g. So, again, if today is April 2026 and let's say I want LTM worth of Revenue and my fiscal year ends in January that means I can go to the most recent 10Q and get the most recent data after the 10-K was filed in January so that will it include January, February and March data (the first quarter's data) and I will add up the revenue from those months.

But that's only the last 3 months worth of revenue- I need 12. 

So I go to the most recent 10-K  (that has 12 months worth of data ending January) and add the revenue from there to the three month revenue I already have but that's too much! 

Because that's 12 months worth of data and we already had 3 months. 

We only needed 9 months worth of data. 

So we have to go back to the 10Q and subtract the first 3 months of the 10-K revenue which is just the 1st quarter data last year and we end up with LTM ending April 2026 revenue!

<Latex formula={`\\text{LTM} = \\text{Most Recent 10-K} + \\text{Current 10-Q} - \\text{Prior Year's Matching 10-Q}`} />

In a more concise format, here are the steps: 

Example: Calculating LTM Revenue in April 2026 (For a Jan. Fiscal Year End)

<ul>
    <li>**Step 1:** Start with the 10-K (+12 months). Take the total Revenue from the most recent 10-K ending January 2026.</li>
    <li>**Step 2:** Add the current 10-Q (+3 months). Add the Revenue from the newest Q1 10-Q (Feb, Mar, Apr 2026). You now have 15 months of data.</li>
    <li>**Step 3:** Subtract the prior year's 10-Q (-3 months). Subtract the Revenue from last year's Q1 10-Q (Feb, Mar, Apr 2025).</li>

    Result = Exactly 12 months. You have successfully swapped out the old 3 months for the new 3 months, leaving you with the Last Twelve Months ending April 2026.

</ul>

<RuleBox align="left">
Def. Purchase Accounting dictates that a company can only report the financial results of an acquired firm for the exact months it officially owned them.
</RuleBox>

For example, if you are Firm A and you buy Firm B in September, assuming you have a December fiscal year-end, you will report only 4 months worth of Firm's B financial results when December comes but you *won't* be able to claim the first 8 months of Firm's B financial results because those results were generated *before* you acquired the firm. So at the end of the day, even if Firm B made $50M that year, your official December 10-K will only show the portion of that money made during those final 4 months.

Now, that's accounting, but we're finance majors and we want annualized numbers not half done numbers. We want the last 12 months of EBITDA for example, **purchase accounting method makes EBITDA lower than it is** so it forces us fiance majors to step in and make an adjustment by adding back those missing 8 months of EBITDA, so we can capture the true, 12-month run-rate of the combined firms as if we had owned them since day one of the fiscal year, giving us a realistic baseline to actually value the company.

The idea is that it's not the norm to have 4 months of EBITDA for firm B so you don't want to carry that assumptions into future years when doing valuation.
