# Motivation 

**Q:** Why find the inverse of a matrix?

**A:** It's another way to solve for **x** in A**x** = **b**. 

See, if we isolate **x** we get, 

<Latex formula = "\mathbf{x} = \frac{\mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{A}}"/>

Do you remember that 1 over anything is just another way to say opposite or **inverse**? So, 

<Latex formula = "\frac{1}{\mathbf{A}} \text{ is the same as } \mathbf{A}^{-1}"/>

therefore,

<Latex formula = "\mathbf{x} = \frac{\mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{A}} \quad \text{is the same as} \quad \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{A}^{-1} \quad"/>

(Think of how negative exponents work, if they're negative they go down under the fraction. Here, we're going in reverse-we're brining the A from under so we'll have to make the exponent negative.)

So, if we find the inverse of matrix A, we can multiply by our vector **b** and get our vector **x**. 

<hr/>

Finding the inverse of a matrix depends on the type of matrix you have. There are two cases. You either are finding the inverse of a (2 x 2) matrix or not. 

For a (2 x 2) there is a formula. 

For all other matricies, we do row reduction. 

Let's get started.

# Practice 

Find the inverse of the following matricies A.

<Problem n = "1">

<Latex formula = "A = \begin{bmatrix} 
M_{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & M_{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & 0 & M_{3} & 0 \\ 
0 & 0 & 0 & M_{4} 
\end{bmatrix}"/>

</Problem>

<Solution>
<Latex formula = "A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 
\frac{1}{M_{1}} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & \frac{1}{M_{2}} & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & 0 & \frac{1}{M_{3}} & 0 \\ 
0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{M_{4}} 
\end{bmatrix}"/>

</Solution>

<Problem n = "2">

<Latex formula = "A = \begin{bmatrix} 
R & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & 0 & R & 1 \\ 
0 & 0 & 0 & 1 
\end{bmatrix}"/>


</Problem>

<Solution>
<Latex formula = "A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 
\frac{1}{R} & -\frac{1}{R} & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 
0 & 0 & \frac{1}{R} & -\frac{1}{R} \\ 
0 & 0 & 0 & 1 
\end{bmatrix}"/>
</Solution>
