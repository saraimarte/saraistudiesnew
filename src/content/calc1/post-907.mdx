Sometimes, when solving limits we get indeterminte forms like so,

∞ ⋅ 0, ∞-∞, 1<sup>∞</sup>, 0<sup>0</sup>, ∞<sup>0</sup> 

Indeterminte form does not mean the limit doesn't exist, it means we simply are unable to determine it. 

But it *can* be determined with a little cool trick.

The trick is if we get the indeterminte form to look like a fraction like 0/0 or ∞/∞ then we can simply take the derivative of the top and bottom functions and find the limit of that result. That's called L'Hopitals Rule.  

Let's try it out!

<Latex formula="\lim\limits_{x \to \infty} \quad x \ln\left(1 - \frac{5}{x^2}\right)" />

<Latex formula="\lim\limits_{x \to \infty} \quad \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(\ln(x))}" />

<Latex formula="\lim\limits_{x \to \infty} \frac{8x}{8x + 1}" />

Sol: 1
