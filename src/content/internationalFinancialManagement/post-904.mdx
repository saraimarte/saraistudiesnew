# The <Pop t = "stock">Equity</Pop> Market Structure 

The stock market consists of two markets: 

<ul>
    <li>**Primary Market:** The company (the issuer) sells new shares of stock directly to investors for the first time to raise capital.</li>
    <li>**Secondary Market:** This is where investors trade shares they already own with other investors. Most people who 'buy stock' are buying it here. In this market, the buyers and sellers are represented by a broker (e.g. Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Vanguard, etc.)</li>
</ul>

The secondary market serves two major purposes:

<ul>
    <li>**Marketability:** it ensures if you buy a share today, you aren't stuck with it forever- you can sell it to someone else whenever you want.</li>
    <li>**Share Valuation:** by having thousands of people buying and selling, the market discovers what the stock is actually worth at any given second.</li>
</ul>

# More on the Secondary Market 

## Types of Orders

Again, in the secondary market, the buyers and sellers are represented by a broker (e.g. Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Vanguard, etc.)

Orders submitted to the broker can be either a: 

<ul>
    <li>**Market Order:** allows you to put in an order to buy or sell shares at whatever the market price is right now. </li>
    <li>**Limit Order:** allows you to put in an order to buy or sell shares at whatever the market price is as long as it doesn't violate the limit you set.</li>
</ul>

E.g. Apple is currently trading around $190. You want to buy 1 share of apple.  

<ul>
    <li>**Market Order:** You tell the broker, "Buy me 1 share of Apple now." You get the share immediately at whatever the market price is once the order goes through (e.g., $190.05).</li>

    <li>**Limit Order:** You tell the broker, "Buy me 1 share of Apple only if the price hits $185." Your order sits and waits. If Apple never drops to $185, you don't buy the share and you might miss the opportunity because it could have dropped to $186 which is still a lower price but because you set the limit at $185, it won't buy it and then the price may go up to $200 and you missed the opportunity to buy at $186.</li>
</ul>

## How Orders are Processed / The Players in the Market 

A broker (like Fidelity) just passes orders to either a dealer or an agency. 

Two types of trading mechanisms: 

<ul>
    <li>A **Dealer** (e.g. NASDAQ) owns the stock themselves so they can fulfill your buy or sell order using their own inventory.</li>
    <li>An **Agency** (e.g. NYSE) does not own the sock but have agents that will match your buy order with a sell order from another investor.</li>
</ul>

Note that exchanges can operate as dealers or agencies. 

# Market Consolidations and Mergers 

The main idea here is that the global stock market is moving away from many small, national exchanges toward a few massive, merged international powerhouses in order to be efficient and increase reach of international stocks. 

For example, in 2000, the Amsterdam, Brussels, and Paris exchanges merged to form Euronext.

Then, Euronext acquired Lisbon, Irish and Oslo exchanges. 

Finally, in 2007, the NYSE acquired Euronext. 

# Trading in International Equities

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **Cross-listing** refers to a firm listing its equity shares on one or more foreign exchanges in addition to its home country stock exchange.
</RuleBox>

A prime example is Toyota, which is a Japanese firm that cross-lists its shares on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) to access U.S. investors.

## Why Companies Cross-List

<ul>

<li>Expand the investor base for a firm's stock</li>
<li>Establish name recognition of the company</li>
<li>Expose the firm to more investor and consumer groups</li>
<li>Signal improved corporate governance</li>
<li>Mitigate the possibility of a hostile takeover</li>

</ul>

## Cross-Border Investing Tools  (Ways to list your shares in another country)

**1. Foreign Stock (Yankee Stock if in America):** Foreign stock sold directly to US investors, denominated in USD, and following strict US SEC/GAAP rules (same idea as Yankee Bond but for Stocks.)

**2. Depository Receipts (American Depository Receipts/ADRs if listed in America):** A receipt representing shares of a foreign stock held in a bank. This is a faster, cheaper way for foreign firms to list in the US.  
 
**Types of ADRs**

There are two types of American Depository Receipts.

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Sponsored ADR** is created by a bank at the specific request of the foreign company that issued the underlying security. This is the only type of ADR that is allowed to be listed on major U.S. stock exchanges like the NYSE or NASDAQ.

OR The foreign company wants to be in the U.S. They hire the U.S. bank, sign the paperwork, and agree to follow U.S. rules so they can trade on big exchanges like the NYSE.

</RuleBox>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** An **Unsponsored ADR** is created at the request of a U.S. investment bank without the direct involvement or consent of the foreign issuing firm. Because the company isn't involved, these can only trade "Over-the-Counter" (OTC) and not on major exchanges.

OR The foreign company doesn't care about the U.S. market. However, a U.S. investment bank sees that Americans want the stock, so the bank creates the receipts on its own without the company's help. These can't trade on big exchanges.
</RuleBox>

# iShares MSCI 

<RuleBox>
**Def.** An **Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)** is a security that tracks an index (like a list of foreign stocks) but trades on a stock exchange like a single stock. It allows you to buy a diversified basket of investments in one go.
</RuleBox>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **iShares** MSCI are Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) managed by <Pop t = "the world's largest asset manager">BlackRock</Pop> that are designed to track the performance of various MSCI stock indexes. They trade on major U.S. exchanges (NYSE and AMEX), offering investors a low-cost, simplified way to hold a diversified portfolio of international stocks.
</RuleBox>


Imagine you want to buy snacks from 50 different countries.

<ul>
    <li>**The Hard Way:** You have to fly to each country, find a grocery store, and buy one snack at a time. This is expensive and takes forever.</li>

    <li>**The ETF Way:** You go to a local store and buy one pre-packaged gift basket that already contains one snack from each of those 50 countries.</li>
</ul>

Buying that one basket is like buying an ETF. You own a little piece of everything inside it with just one purchase.

In this chapter, the snacks are the international stocks listed on an MSCI Index.

MSCI makes the list of what should be in the basket (the Index).

BlackRock (most known for their iShares) builds the physical basket (the ETF) and sells it to you.

You can buy or sell it at any time during the day, just like you would buy a single share of Apple or Toyota.

The price of the basket goes up or down based on how the snacks (the international stocks) inside are doing.

# Factors Affecting International Equity Return

**1. Macroecnomic Factors:** interest rates and unemployment rate can affect on stock return but it tends to have a more localized impact than international.   

**2. Exchange Rates/Currency Risk:** low corelation between exchange rates and stock returns but still positive. Emerging markets are affected the most by exchange rates.  

**3. Industry Factors:** any risks on a particular industry can affect return on stock. The affect is more prevalent in developed markets and less prevalent in emerging markets.  

**4. Market Factors:** these involve specific stock traits like Size (market cap), Value (cheapness), and Momentum (recent price trends). Research shows strong <Pop t = "how much extra return investors get for buying cheap stocks instead of expensive ones.">value premiums</Pop> globally and strong momentum returns everywhere except Japan. 

**5. Country Specific Risk:** this refers to Political Risk, such as the possibility of a local government taking over (expropriating) assets, changing tax policies, or restricting your ability to move currency out of the country.
 
**6. Imperfect Exchange Rate Risk Hedging:** It is very difficult to perfectly protect (hedge) your investment from currency swings because stock returns are variable. Since you don't know exactly what your profit will be, you can't know exactly how much currency protection to buy.

<Latex formula={`r_{Dom} = (1 + r_{For}) \\left( \\frac{E_1}{E_0} \\right) - 1`} />
<Latex formula={`r_{For} = (1 + r_{Dom}) \\left( \\frac{E_0}{E_1} \\right) - 1`} />
<Latex formula={`E_1 = E_0 \\left( \\frac{1 + r_{Dom}}{1 + r_{For}} \\right)`} />
