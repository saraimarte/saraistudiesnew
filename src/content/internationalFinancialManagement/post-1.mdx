# Why Study International Finance?

Well, we don't just do finance domestically. We trade, invest, produce and consume in other countries so we should be educated in the study of those economic factors between countries- that study is called **international finance.**

# What Sets International Finance Apart From Domestic Finance? 

Once you cross the domestic border of finance, there are four new things that come up:

**1. Foreign Exchange Risk:** the risk that fluctuating exchange rates between different national currencies will unexpectedly change the value of your assets, liabilities, or profits.

**2. Political Risks:** you now must account for the politics/rules of other nations that may impact tax laws, limitations in manufacturing, immigration, trade restrictions, war, etc.   

**3. Market Imperfections:** other nation's markets may not be as perfect as yours. They may have high transaction costs, legal barriers, discriminatory taxation, labor markets, etc.   

**4. Expanded Opportunity Set:**  you now have more opportunities to make money or reduce your risk.    

# Goals for International Financial Management

The goals of international financial management varies for different nations:

**1. United States:** maximize shareholder wealth.

**2. Continental Europe:** maximize shareholder wealth but stakeholders include employees, suppliers, customers, and banks, etc. 

**3. Japan:** maximize the value and growth of *keiretsu*- a family or network of independent firms (the idea is similar to a holding company.)  

In this class, we look at things from the U.S. perspective so our goal in international financial management is to maximize shareholder value.

# Globalization of the World Economy: Major Trends and Developments 

**1. Emergence of Globalized Financial Markets:** in 1980, major deregulations of financial markets began, we had advances in technology, and the concept of currency futures and options, multi-currency bonds, cross-border stock listings, international mutual funds came about 

**2. Emergence of the Euro as a Global Currency:** more than 340 million Europeans in 21 countries, are allowed to use the same currency since 1999. Before 1999, each of the 21 countries used a different currency. Now, it's easier to trade, transact and there are lower costs overall. 

<ul>

    <li>For some context, the way this 'Euro Club' works is that in order to be a nation in this club you have to follow certain rules. One rule was that each nation *can* have their own debt but if it does, it has to have a ratio of a <Pop t = "occurs when nations spend more than what they are bringing in/the amount they overspend in a single year">budget deficit</Pop> to GDP of no more than 3%.</li>
    <li>Why was this rule created? So that nations won't take in so much debt to the point they can't afford to pay it back which then decreases the value of the euro for every other nation in the club. But guess what? One nation didn't follow this rule...</li>

</ul>

**3. Europe's Sovereign Debt Crisis of 2010:** in 2009 Greece announced that their budget deficit would be 12.7% of GDP... which is definitely way over than the 3% that it should be. 

<ul>

    <li>This caused investors to sell off Greek government bonds and the rating agencies downgraded them to junk. This is bad because it sent a signal that Greece couldn't pay off it's loans and that other nations in the Euro might be next.</li>
    <li>Because Greece couldn't print its own money to pay off the debt, it had to beg the 'Euro Club' for a bailout, which came with the very strict condition that Greece would have to stop spending as much and start collecting more. This forced Greece to cut retirement checks, hike taxes, cut government worker's salaries, sell state-owned assets like airports, islands, etc. (this is called privatization), and cut hospital's budgets. </li>
    <li>This crisis revealed a major weakness of the Euro as a common currency: Nations in the Euro have achieved monetary integration but without fiscal integration. This means that they all agree to use the same currency (the Euro) but they have different collecting and spending rules like they all have different ways to collect taxes and spend money on roads, schools or military. </li>
</ul>

## Global Level Trade Agreements 

<ul>

    <li>**GATT (1947):** The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade was a multilateral agreement that reduced many trade barriers like tariffs, import quotas, discriminatory taxes, and paperwork. </li>
    <li>**WTO:** The World Trade Organization replaced GATT with more power to actually enforce the rules and make new rulings.  </li>

</ul>

## Regional Level Agreements

<ul>

    <li>**The European Union (EU):** a group of 27 member states that don't have to pay taxes or have any barriers when they are trading among themselves. People can also move, work, and live in any of the 27 countries without needing a special visa.</li>
    <li>**NAFTA to USMCA:** modernized the old NAFTA agreement by adding new rules for digital trade and tightening requirements for the auto industry to ensure more parts and high-wage labor come from North America.</li>
</ul>

## Recent & Mega Trade Deals

<ul>

    <li>**TPP (Trans-Pacific Partnership):** a 2018 deal among 11 Pacific Rim countries (500 million people) designed to slash tariffs. The U.S. initially joined but later withdrew.</li>
    <li>**AfCFTA (African Continental Free Trade Agreement):** a massive agreement covering 49 African countries and 1.2 billion people aimed at stimulating investment and lowering migration barriers.</li>
    <li>**RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership):** signed in 2020,created the world's largest trade bloc by eliminating tariffs and unifying trade rules across 15 Asia-Pacific nations, including the first-ever shared trade agreement between China, Japan, and South Korea. </li>


</ul>
