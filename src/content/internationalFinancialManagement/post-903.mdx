# What is a Bond?

There are different types of debt instruments like loans, notes and bonds. 

<RuleBox>
**Def**. A **Bond** is a debt instrument with a long-term maturity, typically expiring in more than 10 years. 

We call the entity that borrows money the **issuer** and the entity that lends the money, the **bondholder.**  

The <Pop t = "face value or par value">**Principal**</Pop> is the actual amount of money being borrowed. It has a principal and coupon.

The **Coupon** is the periodic interest payment the bond issuer pays to the investor.  

</RuleBox>

E.g. If you buy a $1,000 bond that pays 5% a year:

<ul>
    <li>The Principal is the $1,000.</li>

    <li>The Interest is the $50 you get paid every year.</li>
</ul>

(Notes are bonds but the maturity is shorter. Loans are private.) 

# The Bond Market Structure

The bond market consists of two markets: 

<ul>
    <li>**Primary Market:** Bonds are bought directly from sellers.</li>
    <li>**Secondary Market:** Bonds are bought and sold between investors through a broker.</li>
</ul>

</ul>

# Categorizing Bonds 

Before talking about the different types of bonds, we can categorize any bond based on where it is issued and what currency it uses. 

There are two main categories we will cover: foreign bonds and Eurobonds. 

## Foreign Bonds and Eurobonds

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Foreign Bond** is <Pop t = "sold">issued</Pop>by a foreign company in a domestic market and is denominated in the local currency of that market.
</RuleBox>

E.g. You are in the U.S. and a foreign company, Toyota, issues a bond in the U.S. in the local currency- U.S. dollars. 

<ul>
    <li>**Foreign Bonds have nicknames** depending on where they are issued.</li>
    <li>If a foreign bond is issued in the U.S. it's called a Yankee Bond. If it's issued in Japan it's called a Samurai Bond, etc.</li>
</ul>


<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Eurobond** is issued in a currency that is different from the local currency of the country where it is being sold.
</RuleBox>

E.g. You are in the U.S. and a Japanese company, Toyota, issues a bond in the U.S., but the bond is denominated in a foreign currency—Japanese Yen OR any other currency that is not USD so British Pound for example. 

### National Security Regulations

**Companies prefer to issue Eurobonds instead of Foreign Bonds** because of the red tape that comes with foreign bonds. 

Foreign bonds must follow local rules, which is expensive and slow. 

For example, if a Japanese company wants to issue a bond in U.S. currency, the company can either issue it in the US or in another place. 

If they issue the bond in the U.S. (so a foreign bond), and would have to register their bond with the SEC. 

If they issue the bond it in another place like London, (so a Euro bond), they would not have to register with the SEC even though the currency they are dealing with is USD. 

Because the SEC doesn't own the dollar they just have control of the dollar being transacted in the U.S. geography. 

If the company *has* to issue in the U.S., they use loopholes to avoid having to register with the SEC:

<ul>
    <li>**Rule 415 (Shelf Registration):** allows foreign companies to preregister securities and keep them on the 'shelf' to issue whenever the time is right over a 3-year period.</li>
    <li>**Rule 144A:** allows foreign companies to sell the bonds privately to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs) to avoid full SEC disclosure requirements.</li>
</ul>

## The Hybrid: Global Bonds

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Global Bond** is massive bond issued and traded in multiple countries at the exact same time. It is essentially a combination of a domestic/foreign bond and a Eurobond all rolled into one. They are rare because they are a logistical nightmare, but Verizon issued a famous $49 billion one in 2013. 
</RuleBox>

# International Bond Market Credit Ratings

Just like we have a credit score that reflects how well we do paying our debts in time- firms have a credit rating that rates how well they do paying off their bonds. 

They also assign a credit rating to the bond itself based on it's attributes. 

The firms in charge of assigning these credit ratings are <a href = "https://www.fitchratings.com/">Fitch</a>, <a href = "https://www.moodys.com/">Moody's</a>, and <a href = "https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en">S&P</a> ratings.

# International Bond Market Indexes

Since there isn't a single stock exchange for bonds, we use indexes to see if bond prices ae going up or down.

**The two biggest indexes are:**

<ul>
    <li>**J.P. Morgan International Bond Index:** tracks government bonds in developed countries outside the U.S. </li>
    <li>**Bloomberg Global Aggregate Bond Index:** tracks every bond (government, corporate, etc.) from all over the world.</li>
  
</ul>

**Why do we need indexes?**

<ul>
  <li>**Performance Target:** "Did my bond portfolio do better or worse than the market average?"</li>
  <li>**Risk Measure:** To see how volatile interest rates are in different countries.</li>
  <li>**Index-Linked Products:** Many mutual funds or ETFs are built to simply copy these indexes.</li> 
</ul>


**How they are built?**

Unlike stock indexes (which use market cap), bond indexes are built using:

<ul>
    <li>Currency: Grouping bonds by USD, Euro, or Yen.</li>

    <li>Credit Rating: Separating Investment Grade (safe) from High Yield (junk bonds).</li>

    <li>Maturity: Looking at short-term (1-3 years) vs. long-term (10+ years) performance.</li>
</ul>
