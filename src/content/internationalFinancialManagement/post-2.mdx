# Evolution of the International Monetary System

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **legal tender** are coins that must be accepted if offered in payment of debt.  
</RuleBox>

**1. Bimetallism (Before 1875):** when the legal tender was silver and gold but people only ever used silver and hoarded the gold.

**2. Classical Gold Standard (1875 to 1914):** when major nations abandoned silver as a legal tender and tied the value of their currencies to *only* gold at a fixed price (e.g. such as the U.S. setting the dollar at $20.67 per ounce of gold so for the first time we have exchange rates.) 
<ul>

    <li>At this point, however, we are still mostly trading physical gold.</li>
    <li>**Advantages:** it was stable, trustworthy, and we couldn't overprint gold so the money supply can't continue to grow. We would have to go find physical gold to grow our money supply so inflation was in control. </li>
    <li>**Disadvantages:** we couldn't actually grow our money supply easily and no central power that enforced the rules of the game. Nations could just change how much of their currency was worth 1 ounce of gold.</li>

</ul>

**3. Interwar Period (1915 to 1944):** when the gold standard was just not working due to unstable exchange rates and countries making their money worth less so they can steal business from other countries like making it cheaper for other countries to buy goods so their exports increase. 

**4. Bretton Woods System (1945 to 1972)** when 44 nations met in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire on July 1944 to create a new fixed exchange rate system where the U.S. dollar was tied to gold at $35 per ounce, and everybody else tied their money to the dollar so the U.S. dollar dominated as the global currency. 

<ul>

    <li>**Advantages:** lower transaction costs because of the lack of transportation of gold, stable exchange rates.</li>
    <li>**Disadvantages:** triffin paradox is the idea that the Bretton Woods system was doomed because the world needed a constant supply of U.S. dollars to trade, but printing all those dollars eventually made it impossible for the U.S. to guarantee they could all be exchanged for gold.</li>
    <li>In 1971, President Nixon ended the "gold window," meaning the U.S. would no longer trade gold for dollars, which effectively killed the fixed exchange rate system. This lead to an economic theory called the Trilemma.</li>
</ul>

## Trilemma 

<RuleBox align = "left">
**Def.** The **Trilemma** is an economic theory that a country cannot last having all three of these at the same time- it has to pick two:

**1.** A Fixed Exchange Rate

**2.** Free Movement of Capital (money moving in/out of the country easily)

**3.** An Independent Monetary Policy (the ability to set your own interest rates)

</RuleBox>

Some examples of what countries nowadays have picked: 

<ul>
    <li>The U.S. picks #2 and #3, but lets the dollar float (no fixed rate).</li>
    <li>The <Pop t = "'Euro Club'">Eurozone</Pop> picks #1 and #2, but they lose their independent policy (the ECB decides for everyone).</li>
    <li>China often picks #1 and #3, but restricts the free flow of capital by limiting their citizens to exchanging only $50,000 worth of foreign currency per year etc. </li>
</ul>

(We will go over three examples of currency crisis caused by countries violating the Trilemma theory <a href = "#here">here</a>)

Now, since the U.S. couldn't keep enough gold to back all the dollars they were printing- the system broke and that lead us to... 

**5. Flexible Exchange Rate Regime (1973 to Present):** we moved to the **Jamaica Accord (1976)** which officially allowed currencies to float and find their own value based on market demand (gold was also officially abandoned as a legal tender.)

# Other

<Section t = "Current Exchange Rate Arrangements">

According to the International Monetary Fund (IMF), there are exactly ten separate exchange rate regimes or arrangements that a nation can adopt to manage its currency:

**1. No separate legal tender:** where countries have another country's currency as their only legal tender (e.g. Panama uses U.S. dollars as their only legal tender)  

**2. Currency board:** where a country maintains its own currency but backs it entirely with foreign reserves to ensure a strictly fixed exchange rate (e.g. Hong Kong tying its dollar to the U.S. dollar)  

**3. Conventional peg:** a nation formally anchors its currency at a fixed rate to another single currency or a basket of currencies with very little room for movement (e.g. Saudi Arabia pegs the Riyal to the U.S. dollar)

**4. Stabilized arrangement:** like a peg but allows the currency to fluctuate within a slightly wider, pre-defined range, such as ±2% over a six-month period (e.g. Vietnam maintains a stable rate for the Dong)

**5. Crawling peg:** where a country's currency is tied to another but is adjusted by small, regular amounts on a set schedule to keep up with inflation (e.g. Nicaragua uses a pre-set devaluation schedule)  

**6. Crawl-like arrangement:** like a crawling peg but requires the currency value to stay within a narrow margin of a moving trend (e.g. Singapore manages its dollar against a basket of trade partners)

**7. Pegged exchange rate with horizontal bands:** where the country's currency value is kept within specific margins around a central fixed rate that can be updated on a daily basis (e.g. Morocco allows the Dirham to fluctuate within a set band)  

**8. Other managed arrangement:** this includes complex systems with separate rules for different versions of a currency or those that don't fit other categories (e.g. China uses a managed float with onshore and offshore rules)

**9. Floating:** where the market primarily determines the currency's value, but the government uses active intervention programs to slow down sharp increases or decreases (e.g. India or Brazil)

**10. Free floating:** where the exchange rate is determined entirely by market supply and demand with no capital controls and no significant government interference in its value (e.g. United States or United Kingdom)

</Section>

# Emergence of the Euro as a Global Currency

**1. European Monetary System  (1979-1998)**

During the EMS period (1979-1998), countries still had their own individual currencies (like the French Franc, German Mark, and Italian Lira) and were not yet using the Euro.

<RuleBox>
**Def.** The **European Monetary System (EMS)** was launched in 1979 with the following goals:

<ul>
    <li>**To establish a zone of monetary stability in Europe** so that prices would stay stable and trade between neighbors would be more predictable.</li>
    <li>**To coordinate policies against non-EMS currencies** ensuring that European countries acted as a united team when dealing with outsider currencies, specifically to manage how they all stood against the U.S. dollar.</li>
    <li>**To pave the way for monetary union:** This was the warm-up period designed to get countries used to working together so they could eventually ditch their individual national currencies and switch to one shared currency- the Euro.</li>
</ul>
 
</RuleBox>

Two main tools the EMS used were: 

<ul>
    <li>European Currency Unit (ECU)</li>
    <li>Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM)</li>
</ul>

**2. Euro and European Monetary Union (1999):**

<RuleBox>
**Def.** The **European Monetary Union (EMU)** was created in 1999 where 11 of 15 EU countries adopted a common currency- the euro (there are now 21 countries using the Euro.)

<ul>
    <li>**To establish a zone of monetary stability in Europe** so that prices would stay stable and trade between neighbors would be more predictable.</li>
    <li>**To coordinate policies against non-EMS currencies** ensuring that European countries acted as a united team when dealing with outsider currencies, specifically to manage how they all stood against the U.S. dollar.</li>
    <li>**To pave the way for monetary union:** This was the warm-up period designed to get countries used to working together so they could eventually ditch their individual national currencies and switch to one shared currency- the Euro.</li>
</ul>
 
</RuleBox>

<ul>
    <li>**Advantages:** reduced transaction costs, elimination of exchange rate uncertainty, political cooperation in Europe, and enhanced efficiency   </li> 
    <li>**Disadvantages:** loss of national monetary and exchange rate policy independence  </li>
    <li>The euro is the currently the second most important currency in the world</li> 
</ul>


# <h1 id = "here">Currency Crises</h1> 

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **Capital Flight** is a large-scale exodus of money from a country, occurring when investors and citizens suddenly lose confidence in that nation's economy and rush to move their assets into "safer" foreign currencies like the U.S. dollar.
</RuleBox>

The following crisis occurred because these countries all tried to have the 3 components:

**1.** A Fixed Exchange Rate

**2.** Free Movement of Capital (money moving in/out of the country easily)

**3.** An Independent Monetary Policy (the ability to set your own interest rates)

which breaks the Trilemma.

**1. Mexican Peso Crisis (1994 to 1995):**  Mexico attempted to maintain a fixed exchange rate, free movement of capital, and an independent monetary policy, but it failed when massive capital flight forced a sudden 40% devaluation of the peso.    
**2. Asian Currency Crisis (1997 to 1998):** These nations tried to keep their exchange rates fixed while allowing free capital flows and independent policies, but the system collapsed when a sudden panic caused investors to pull their money out, triggering a deep regional recession.     
**3. Argentine Peso Crisis (2002):**  Argentina linked its peso exactly to the U.S. dollar while maintaining free capital movement and independent policy, but it failed because the strengthening dollar made Argentine exports too expensive, leading to a severe economic downturn.  

# Rise of Chinese RMB

<ul>
  <li>**Dual-Currency Structure:** China manages two different versions of its currency: "onshore" (CNY) used within the mainland and "offshore" (CNH) used in international markets.</li>
  <li>**Onshore Restrictions:** The onshore RMB is strictly regulated by daily government fixings and capital controls that prevent it from being moved freely out of the country.</li>
  <li>**Offshore Market:** The offshore RMB is a fully convertible currency whose value is determined by global supply and demand.</li>
  <li>**Arbitrage Barriers:** Even when the onshore and offshore prices move apart, the lack of access to onshore accounts makes it difficult for market players to profit from the difference.</li>
  <li>**The Trinity Choice:** By using these capital controls, China is able to maintain an independent monetary policy and a managed exchange rate at the same time.</li>
</ul>

# Fixed versus Flexible Exchange Rate Regimes

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **Fixed Exchange Rate** is a system where a country's government or central bank ties the value of its currency to another currency or a commodity, such as gold, to maintain a constant price.
</RuleBox>

<ul>
  <li>**Easier External Adjustments:** This regime allows a country's economy to adjust more naturally to external market shifts.</li>
  <li>**National Policy Autonomy:** Governments can use their own monetary policy for their own benefit to help the economy adjust against market pressures.</li>
 </ul>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** **Flexible (Floating) Exchange Rate** is a system where the value of a currency is determined entirely by the supply and demand of the global market rather than being officially set or maintained by the government.
</RuleBox>

<ul>
  <li>**Exchange Rate Uncertainty:** Frequent fluctuations in value create uncertainty in the marketplace.</li>
  <li>**Impact on International Trade:** This uncertainty can potentially hurt international trade because businesses cannot predict future costs.</li>
  <li>**Lack of Crisis Safeguards:** Flexible regimes do not have built-in safeguards to prevent sudden crises, such as massive capital flight.</li>
</ul>

# Criteria for a Good International Monetary System

A successful system, whether fixed or flexible, must provide three key things:

<ul>
  <li>**Liquidity:** A good system must provide a sufficient flow of money to support international trade.</li>
  <li>**Mechanism for Adjustment:** There must be a way for the system to correct itself during economic shifts, a feature which Greece notably lacked during its crisis.</li>
  <li>**Confidence:** The system must maintain high levels of trust to prevent sudden financial crises or capital flight.</li>
</ul>
