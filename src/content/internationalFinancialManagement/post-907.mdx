# International Correlation Structure 

Recall that a portfolio is a group of securities. 

Investors can reduce their portfolio risk if the securities that they invest in are less correlated with one another. 

Securities returns are less correlated if the securities are from different countries than if they are from the same country. 

# Risk Diversification

As you add more stocks to a portfolio, you diversify away individual (idiosyncratic) risk, but you are eventually left with the market risk. Expanding to an international portfolio pushes that baseline market risk down even further.


## Performance Metrics: World Beta and Sharpe Ratio

# Optimal International Portfolio 

The Optimal International Portfolio (OIP) is the exact weighting of global assets that maximizes the Sharpe ratio.

Gains are measured by looking at how much the Sharpe ratio increases, or how much extra return is generated if you adjust the international portfolio to have the exact same risk level as the domestic one.

<ul>
    <li>The U.S. Factor: The U.S. is included in every country's optimal portfolio due to its massive market cap, historically high returns, and low volatility (resulting in the highest historical Sharpe ratio).</li>
    <li>Safe-Haven Currencies: Switzerland, Sweden, and the U.S. are common in optimal portfolios. The professor notes that currencies like the Swiss Franc and U.S. Dollar historically appreciate during global equity sell-offs. This acts as a natural buffer, dampening downside volatility for foreign investors.</li>
    <li>The Hong Kong Peg: Hong Kong is heavily represented in optimal portfolios largely because its currency is pegged to the U.S. Dollar, providing similar safe-haven benefits.</li>
    <li>The Japan Exception: Japan is completely excluded from historical optimal portfolios because it endured a severe, multi-decade period of underperformance, yielding the lowest Sharpe ratio</li>
</ul>
