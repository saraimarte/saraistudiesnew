# What is the FX Market? 

[Video soon]

<RuleBox>
**Def.** The **Foreign Exchange Market (FX/FOREX)** is where currencies are bought and sold. 
</RuleBox>

<ul>

    <li>It's a **24 hour market**</li>
    <li>**It's an OTC market:** Unlike the stock market, which has a central building (like the New York Stock Exchange), the FX market is Over-the-Counter (OTC). Trading happens directly between two parties (like two banks) rather than on a centralized exchange floor.</li>
    <li>**Instruments/Ways to Trade Currency:** spots, forwards, futures, swaps and options (aka derivatives) </li>
    <li>**Participants/Two-tier Market:** the two main participants are in the interbank/wholesale market (where banks like JPMorgan or HSBC trade with each other or non banks like mutual funds, pension funds, or MNCs, etc.), or the retail market (everyone else; small businesses, travelers, individual investors like speculators, etc. trade with each other). They make up 14% of the transactions in the FX market.</li>
    <li>The Foreign Exchange Market is the **largest market globally.**</li>
    <li>**There *is* a need for the FX market** (e.g. if we travel to Europe- we have to change our dollars to euros.) But are there other players in the marketplace? Yes (e.g. speculators.)</li>
    <li>**Dealer/Broker Driven:** The market relies on financial institutions acting as middlemen (broker) to facilitate trades between parties since there is no central place in charge of the FX market like the NYSE is to the stock market.</li>
    <li>**Inconsistent Liquidity:** Because there is no central exchange, the ease of buying or selling can vary depending on the time of day or the specific currency.</li>
    <li>**Limited Visibility:** Prices and trade volumes aren't always public, as trades happen privately between two parties (since it's an OTC market data doesn't have to be public).</li>
    <li>**Credit Risks:** There is a risk that the other party in the trade might not have the funds to complete the deal.</li>
    <li>**Settlement Risks:** The danger that one party delivers the currency but the other fails to send theirs in return.</li>
    <li>**Limited Regulations:** there is way less regulation than the stock market.</li>
    <li>**Non-Centralized:** There is no headquarters like the NYSE; the market exists entirely across a global network of computers (OTC.)</li>
    <li>**Market Maker Preferences for OTC:** Large banks prefer this structure because it allows them to customize deals and keep their trading strategies private.</li>
    <li>**Moving more electronic for spot FX:** While traditionally done over the phone, most immediate (spot) trades are now handled through high-speed electronic platforms.</li>

</ul>
