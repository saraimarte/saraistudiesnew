# What is a Swap?

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Swap** is a custom agreement between two parties to trade cash flows with each other over a set period of time.
</RuleBox>

# Types of Swaps

There are two types of swaps: Interest Rate and Currency Swaps. 

<RuleBox align = "left">
**Def.** An **Interest Rate Swap** is a contract between two parties that agree to swap interest rates. 

Note that both interest rates have to be in the same currency.
</RuleBox>

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Currency Swap** is a contract between two parties that agree to swap principal amounts in different currencies and interest payments (e.g. fixed, float)
</RuleBox>

# Swap Market Statistics

<ul>
    <li>The interest rate swap market is about 10 times larger than the currency swap market.</li>
    <li>The U.S. dollar, Euro, Japanese yen, British pound, and Canadian dollar dominate the global swap market.</li>
    <li>The swap market dropped off a few years ago due to Dodd-Frank regulations (which required more reporting, documentation, and code compression), but it has since recovered to previous highs.</li>
</ul>

# The Swap Bank  

<RuleBox>
**Def.** A **Swap Bank** is a generic term used to describe a financial institution that facilitates swaps between counterparties. 
</RuleBox>

<ul>
    <li>Swap Banks can be international commercial bank, investment bank, merchant bank, or independent operator.</li>
</ul> 

**A swap bank can either serve as a broker or a dealer.**

<ul>
   <li>As a broker, the swap bank matches the counterparties but does not take on any risk of the swap.</li>
   <li>As a dealer, the swap bank actually takes the other side of the trade, taking it into their own inventory until they can lay it off on someone else. They do assume risk.</li>
</ul> 

# Swap Market Quotations  

Note: "against" means "exchanged for"

## What is SOFR?

The market convention in regards to quoting interest rate swaps is to quote against a local standard reference in the same currency. 

Every major currency has its own metric that measures the cost of money in that specific currency.

<ul>
    <li>In the U.S., the standard reference is SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate.)</li>

    <li> In Europe, the standard reference is EURIBOR (Euro Interbank Offered Rate).</li>

    <li> In Japan, it's TONA (Tokyo Overnight Average Rate).</li>
</ul> 

E.g. For example, a Swap Bank in an interest rate swap will quote two prices to lock in a profit (this is called the bid-ask spread):

<ul>
    <li><b>The Buy Price (The Bid):</b> The bank agrees to <b>pay</b> a fixed rate of 6.60% against receiving the floating SOFR.</li>
    <li><b>The Sell Price (The Ask):</b> The bank agrees to <b>receive</b> a higher fixed rate of 6.70% against paying the floating SOFR.</li>
</ul>

**The Takeaway:** The bank forces a gap between what it collects (6.70%) and what it pays out (6.60%). That 0.10% difference is the bank's guaranteed fee for setting up the trade.

## Quality Spread Differential (QSD) Calculations

<RuleBox align = "left">
**Def.** **Quality Spread Differential (QSD)** is the total amount of money saved by doing the swap.

QSD = (Difference in Fixed Rates) - (Difference in Floating Rates)

**The Rule:** If the QSD is positive, the swap makes sense. If it's zero or negative, the swap is useless.

</RuleBox>
